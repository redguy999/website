The October Revolution

Preface

Welcome to the New York times archives. I am researching 1917 Russian history. I could use your help in uncovering records related to the October Revolution. However, before we dive into the archives, it will be helpful to review some Russian history prior to October, the timeline below will help. As 1917 dawns over Eastern Europe World War I is raging. It does not end, for Russia until March 3, 1918 when the Treaty of Brest-Litovsky is signed. Compulsory military service had been mandated shortly before World War I broke out and continued throughout 1917. On March 8, 1917 protest broke out in Petrograd (St. Petersburg) involving peasants, striking workers and soldiers, all of whom were protesting the lack of basic necessities. Many Russians were starving. March 15, 1917 Tsar Nicholas II abdicated and his brother Grand Duke Michael refused to take the throne. This was the end of the Romanov dynasty and the beginning of a series of provisional governments. April 16 Vladamir Lenin returned to Russia. July 16-20 became known as the July Days in which the workers and soldiers conducted armed demonstrations against the provisional government because of the deplorable living conditions. Following these protests a new provisional government was established with Alekander Kerensky as the head. Lenin was accused of working with the Germans and was forced to flee to Finland. Note: Kerensky is associated with the Soviets, which is the Russian word for an elected council, much like a legislature. Lenin was associated with the Bolsheviks. The military comprised a third group, separate from the Soviet troops which were loyal to Kerensky. September 9 a military coup d’etat was attempted, led by Lavr Georgiyevich Kornilov, commander and chief of the Russian army. Kornilov was attempting to restrain the Bolsheviks. Kerensky and his Soviet troops stopped Kornilov and his Russian troops. Kornilov’s actions strengthened popular support for the Bolsheviks. October 20 some scholars argue that Lenin, in disguise snuck back into Russia. However, other scholars challenge this story. November 7-8th the October Revolution occurred. (It is called the October revolution because it occurred on October 24-25 of the Julian Calendar, which is the old calendar.)

These protests and revolutions show how the Russian people resisted unfair government rules, laws and actions. The protesting, strikes, and movements were fine, the Russian people were unhappy about the food shortages and wanted the government to know about it. The revolution, however, was a step too far, because it was unnessesary violence (violence is almost never nessesary, this is one of those times), and it was done only because the provisional government couldn't provide a solution, not because there was a better solution that the people knew. The revolution was shortsighted, and didn't solve anything, in fact it just created more problems, like needing to make a new government, who runs this government, how it is run, and several other issues that no one was really ready to solve.
